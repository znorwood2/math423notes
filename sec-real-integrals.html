    Section 6.2 Applications to real integrals

    Between two truths of the real domain, the easiest and shortest path quite often passes through the complex domain.
    ―Jacques Hadamard
    A typical appliaction of the Residue Theorem is to evaluate an improper integral \(\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\,dx\) from calculus. Recall that this equals \(\lim\limits_{R\to \infty} \int_{-R}^R f(x)\,dx\) when the improper integral exists.

    Example 6.2.1.

    Here is the prototypical example. We evaluate the integral \(\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{1}{1+x^2}\,dx\text{.}\) Let \(C_R\) be the semicircular closed curve that follows the straight-line path from \(-R\) to \(R\) and then the semicircle in the upper halfplane from \(R\) to \(-R\text{.}\) Notice that for sufficiently large \(R\text{,}\) the singularity \(i\) lies inside the closed curve \(C_R\text{.}\) So we can evaluate \(\oint_{C_R} \frac{1}{1+z^2}\,dz\) using the Residue Theorem:
    \begin{equation*} \oint_{C_R} \frac{1}{1+z^2}\,dz = 2\pi i \Res\limits_{z=i} \frac{1}{1+z^2} = 2\pi i \frac{1}{2i} = \pi. \end{equation*}
    What's left is to integrate over the arc \(A_R\) that follows the semicircle from \(R\) to \(-R\text{.}\) We are going to do an ML-estimate to argue that this integral tends to \(0\) in modulus as \(R\to \infty\text{.}\) Notice that for \(z\) on \(A_R\text{,}\) we have \(\abs{z} = R\) and so \(\abs{z^2 + 1} \ge R^2 - 1\text{.}\) (Draw a picture of the arc and the arc shifted over by \(1\) unit to the right.) Taking reciprocals gives, for \(z\in A_R\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\frac{1}{1+z^2}} = \frac{1}{\abs{1+z^2}} \le \frac{1}{R^2-1}. \end{equation*}
    Now we can do the ML-estimate:
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\int_{A_R} \frac{1}{1 + z^2}\,dz} \le \frac{1}{R^2 - 1}\cdot \pi R \underset{R\to \infty}{\longrightarrow} 0. \end{equation*}
    Now we can compute the improper integral we started with:
    \begin{equation*} \int_{-R}^R\frac{1}{1+x^2}\,dx = \oint_{C_R} \frac{1}{1+z^2}\,dz - \int_{A_R} \frac{1}{1+z^2}\,dz = \pi - \int_{A_R}\frac{1}{1+z^2}\,dz \underset{R\to\infty}{\longrightarrow} \pi. \end{equation*}
    So \(\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{1}{1+x^2}\,dx = \pi\text{.}\)
    Of course, you could have done this example using methods from calculus. But we will see other examples that cannot be solved without complex methods.
    The same technique applies more generally:

    Fact 6.2.2.

    Suppose that \(P(x),Q(x)\) are polynomials, \(Q\) has no real roots, and \(\deg Q(x) \ge \deg P(x) + 2\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\,dx = 2\pi i \sum \Res_{z=a} \frac{P(z)}{Q(z)}, \end{equation*}
    where the sum on the right is taken over all poles \(a\) of \(P/Q\) in the upper halfplane.

    Example 6.2.3.

    Let \(a \gt 0\) be a real parameter and compute \(\displaystyle \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{\cos(ax)}{1 + x^2}\,dx\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    As in the previous example, let \(A_R\) be the semicircular path that proceeds from \(R\) through \(Ri\) to \(-R\text{,}\) and let \(C_R\) be the closed path that combines \(A_R\) with the straight-line path from \(-R\) to \(R\text{.}\)
    For a non-obvious reason, we will try replacing \(\cos(ax)\) by \(e^{iaz}\) instead of \(\cos(a z)\text{.}\) Use the Residue Theorem to compute the integral over \(C_R\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \oint_{C_R} \frac{e^{iaz}}{1 + z^2}\,dz = 2\pi i \Res_{z=i} \frac{e^{iaz}}{1 + z^2} = 2\pi i \left( \frac{e^{iaz}}{2z} \left.\right|_{z = i} \right) = \pi e^{-a} \end{equation*}
    Next we deal with the integral over \(A_R\) using an ML estimate. For \(z\) in the upper halfplane,
    \begin{equation*} \abs{e^{iaz}} = e^{ia(x + iy)} = e^{ay} \le 1, \end{equation*}
    since \(a \gt 0\text{.}\) So the ML estimate can be done almost exactly as in the previous example:
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\int_{A_R} \frac{e^{iaz}}{1 + z^2}\,dz} \le \frac{1}{R^2 - 1}\pi R \underset{R\to\infty}{\longrightarrow} 0. \end{equation*}
    Just as in the previous example we must have
    \begin{equation*} \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{e^{iax}}{1 + x^2}\,dx = \pi e^{-a}. \end{equation*}
    Recalling Definition 4.1.3, we take real parts to get
    \begin{equation*} \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{\cos(ax)}{1 + x^2}\,dx = \pi e^{-a}. \end{equation*}
    (We could take imaginary parts to see that \(\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{\sin(ax)}{1 + x^2}\,dx = 0\text{,}\) but that isn't interesting since it is an integral of an odd function over a symmetric interval.)
    And now for something much different.

    Example 6.2.4.

    Let \(a \gt 1\) be a real parameter, and evaluate the integral \(\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{a + \cos\theta}\,d\theta\text{.}\) (Notice that this is a real calculus integral.)
    Solution.
    Recalling Definition 4.1.3, we recognize this integral as a contour integral over the unit circle parametrized by \(\gamma(\theta) = e^{i \theta},\) \(0 \le \theta \le 2\pi\text{.}\) You can think of this as a substitution or change of variables:
    \begin{equation*} z = e^{i\theta}, \quad z = iz d\theta, \quad d\theta = \frac{1}{iz}dz \end{equation*}
    What remains to replace in the integrand is the \(\cos\theta\text{.}\) For \(\theta\in [0,2\pi]\text{,}\) \(z = e^{i\theta}\) is on the unit circle, so we have
    \begin{equation*} \cos\theta = \frac{e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}}{2} = \frac{z + \frac{1}{z}}{2}. \end{equation*}
    Now we are ready to do the substitution:
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{a + \cos\theta}\,d\theta = \oint_{\abs{z} = 1} \frac{\frac{dz}{iz}}{a + \frac{z + 1/z}{2}} = \frac{2}{i} \oint_{\abs{z} = 1} \frac{dz}{z^2 + 2az + 1} \end{equation*}
    The poles of the integrand (by the Quadratic Formula) occur at \(-a \pm \sqrt{a^2 - 1}\text{.}\) Only \(a + \sqrt{a^2 - 1}\) lies inside the unit circle, so that's the one we need for the Residue Theorem.
    \begin{equation*} \Res_{z = -a + \sqrt{a^2 - 1}} \frac{1}{z^2 + 2az + 1} = \frac{1}{2z + 2a} \left.\right|_{z = -a + \sqrt{a^2 - 1}} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{a^2 - 1}} \end{equation*}
    An application of the Residue Theorem finishes the computation:
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{d\theta}{a + \cos\theta} = \frac{2}{i}\cdot 2\pi i \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{a^2 - 1}} = \frac{2\pi}{\sqrt{a^2 - 1}} \end{equation*}

    Subsection 6.2.1 Fractional residues and principal values of divergent integrals

    We have many tools for computing integrals around a whole circle; what about an arc of a circle, e.g. a semicircle?

    Theorem 6.2.5. The Fractional Residue Theorem.

    Suppose that \(f\) has a simple pole at \(a\text{.}\) For \(\eps \gt 0,\) let \(A_\eps\) be the arc centered at \(a\) of radius \(\eps\) subtending an angle of \(\beta - \alpha\) radians, parametrized by \(a + \eps e^{i\theta},\) \(\theta \in [\alpha,\beta]\text{.}\) In the limit as \(\eps \to 0\text{,}\) we have the following generalization of the Residue Theorem.
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{\eps \to 0} \int_{A_\eps} f(z)\,dz = (\beta - \alpha) i \Res_{z = a}f(z) \end{equation*}
    (Notice that the case \(\alpha = 0\text{,}\) \(\beta = 2\pi\) follows by applying the Residue Theorem and taking a limit. We will primarily be interested in the case \(\alpha = 0\text{,}\) \(\beta = \pi\text{.}\))

    Proof.

    Since \(f\) has a simple pole at \(a\text{,}\) its Laurent series expansion near \(a\) looks like this:
    \begin{equation*} f(z) = \frac{c_{-1}}{z - a} + c_0 + c_1(z-a) + \cdots \end{equation*}
    We simply use the parametrization of the arc and integrate term by term. Most of the terms will go to \(0\) as \(\eps \to 0\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \int_{A_\eps} f(z)\,dz \amp = \int_\alpha^\beta f(a + \eps e^{i\theta})\epsilon i e^{i\theta}\,d\theta \\ \amp = \int_\alpha^\beta \left( c_{-1}i + c_0 i \epsilon e^{i\theta} + c_1 i \eps^2 e^{2i\theta} + \cdots \right) \, d\theta \\ \amp = \int_\alpha^\beta c_{-1}i\,d\theta + \eps \int_\alpha^\beta (c_0 i e^{i\theta} + \cdots)\\ \amp \underset{\eps \to 0}{\longrightarrow} (\beta - \alpha)i \Res_{z=a} f(z) \end{align*}
    (principal values)
