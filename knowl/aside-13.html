A function \(f\colon\R^2\to\R^2\) can be decomposed into two component functions \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) so that \(f(x,y) = (f_1(x,y),f_2(x,y))\text{.}\) Notice that \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) are functions \(\R^2\to\R\text{:}\) their graphs are surfaces, just like in multivariable calculus. Then \(f\) is real-differentiable if and only if both \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) are differentiable (meaning that they have well-defined tangent planes). (This is not usually taken as the definition, but it is equivalent.)
