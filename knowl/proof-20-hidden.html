We'll give the proofs of all the easy directions here and leave the difficult direction for a separate proposition.
First, notice that we already observed that Item a implies Item b after our discussion of the FTC for contour integrals. It is not too difficult to see that conditions Item b and Item c are equivalent. Assume Item b and suppose that \(\gamma\) is closed. Divide \(\gamma\) into two paths, \(\gamma_1\) from \(z_1\) to \(z_2\) and \(\gamma_2\) from \(z_2\) back to \(z_1\text{.}\) (It does not matter where this division happens.) Using path-independence and the basic properties of contour integrals, we have
\begin{equation*} \int_{\gamma_1} f(z)\,dz = \int_{\gamma_2} f(z)\,dz = -\int_{\gamma_2} f(z)\,dz, \end{equation*}
from which we conclude that
\begin{equation*} \int_\gamma f(z)\,dz = \int_{\gamma_1 + -\gamma_2} f(z)\,dz = 0. \end{equation*}
So Item b implies Item c. The proof of the converse uses similar ideas and is left as an exercise.
So it remains to show that Item b implies Item a, which we leave to Checkpoint 4.3.4 because it will be useful to have a refinement of this result.
