    Section 4.3 Cauchy's Theorem

    Theorem 4.3.1.

    Suppose that \(f\) is a continuous function defined on a domain \(D\text{.}\) The following are equivalent:
    1. \(f\) has an antiderivative \(F\) on \(D\text{;}\)
    2. for all \(z_1,z_2\in D\) the integral \(\int_{z_1}^{z_2} f(z)\,dz\) is independent of path, meaning that for all contours \(\gamma,\delta\colon [a,b]\to D\) with \(\gamma(a) = \delta(a) = z_1\) and \(\gamma(b) = \delta(b) = z_2\) we have \(\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz = \int_\delta f(z)\,dz\text{;}\)
    3. for all closed paths \(\gamma \colon [a,b]\to D\) we have \(\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 0\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    We'll give the proofs of all the easy directions here and leave the difficult direction for a separate proposition.
    First, notice that we already observed that Item¬†a implies Item¬†b after our discussion of the FTC for contour integrals. It is not too difficult to see that conditions Item¬†b and Item¬†c are equivalent. Assume Item¬†b and suppose that \(\gamma\) is closed. Divide \(\gamma\) into two paths, \(\gamma_1\) from \(z_1\) to \(z_2\) and \(\gamma_2\) from \(z_2\) back to \(z_1\text{.}\) (It does not matter where this division happens.) Using path-independence and the basic properties of contour integrals, we have
    \begin{equation*} \int_{\gamma_1} f(z)\,dz = \int_{\gamma_2} f(z)\,dz = -\int_{\gamma_2} f(z)\,dz, \end{equation*}
    from which we conclude that
    \begin{equation*} \int_\gamma f(z)\,dz = \int_{\gamma_1 + -\gamma_2} f(z)\,dz = 0. \end{equation*}
    So Item¬†b implies Item¬†c. The proof of the converse uses similar ideas and is left as an exercise.
    So it remains to show that Item¬†b implies Item¬†a, which we leave to Checkpoint¬†4.3.4 because it will be useful to have a refinement of this result.
    Notice that when we say that \(f\) has an antiderivative here, we mean that it has a complex antiderivative, i.e., a function \(F\) for which \(\frac{d}{dz} F(z) = f(z)\text{.}\)
    Theorem¬†4.3.1 should remind you of a theorem from multivariable calculus. In fact you can use one to prove the other; see Checkpoint¬†4.4.3.

    Proposition 4.3.2.

    Suppose that \(U\) is a star-shaped domain, \(f\colon U\to \C\) is continuous, and \(\int_{\partial T} f(z)\,dz = 0\) for all triangles \(T \subseteq U\text{.}\) Then \(f\) has an antiderivative on \(U\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(a_0 \in U\) be a point of \(U\) with respect to which \(U\) is star-shaped. For \(w\in U\) define \(F(w) = \int_{[a_0,w]} f(z)\,dz\text{,}\) where \([a_0,w]\) is the straight-line path from \(a_0\) to \(w\text{.}\) (Notice that \([a_0,w]\) lies completely in \(U\text{,}\) since \(U\) is star-shaped wrt \(a_0\text{.}\))
    We aim to show that \(F'(w) = f(w)\text{.}\) Since \(U\) is open, there is \(\epsilon \gt 0\) for which \(B_\epsilon(w) \subseteq U\text{.}\) Let \(\Delta w \lt \epsilon\text{.}\) Let \(T\) be the triangle formed by \(a_0, w\text{,}\) and \(w+\Delta w\text{,}\) with boundary oriented counterclockwise. Notice that \(T\) is completely included in \(U\text{,}\) since every point on the line segment \([w,w+\Delta w]\) is (by choice of \(\Delta w\)), and since \(U\) is star-shaped wrt \(a_0\text{.}\) We can therefore apply our assumption to conclude that \(\int_{[a_0,w] + [w,w+\Delta w] + [w+\Delta w,a_0]} f(z)\,dz = 0\text{.}\) On the other hand, this integral also equals \(F(w+\Delta w) - \int_{[w,w + \Delta w]} f(z)\,dz - F(w)\text{,}\) so we rearrange to obtain
    \begin{equation*} F(w + \Delta w) - F(w) = \int_{[w + \Delta w,w]} f(z)\,dz. \end{equation*}
    What we'd really like to estimate is the difference quotient \(\frac{F(w + \Delta w) - F(w)}{\Delta w}\text{,}\) so we rearrange further (noting that \(f(w)\) is a constant function, relative to \(z\)).
    \begin{equation*} F(w + \Delta w) - F(w) - \Delta w f(w) = \int_{[w+\Delta w,w]} (f(z) - f(w))\, dz \end{equation*}
    Now we are in a position to make the final estimate.
    \begin{align*} \abs{\frac{F(w + \Delta w) - F(w)}{\Delta w} - f(w)} \amp \le \frac{1}{\abs{\Delta w}} \abs{\int_{[w + \Delta w, w]} (f(z) - f(w)) \, dz} \\ \amp \le \frac{1}{\abs{\Delta w}} \abs{\Delta w} \sup_{[w + \Delta w,w]} \abs{f(z) - f(w)}, \end{align*}
    which tends to \(0\) as \(\Delta w\) tends to \(0\text{,}\) since \(f\) is continuous.

    Checkpoint 4.3.4.

    Redo the proof of Proposition¬†4.3.2 without the assumption that \(U\) is star-shaped, but assuming that \(\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 0\) for all closed contours \(\gamma\) (not only triangles) in \(U\text{.}\) (Choose \(a_0\) to be any element of \(U\text{,}\) and take \([a_0,w]\) to be any path from \(a_0\) to \(w\) that lies completely inside \(U\text{.}\))
    Make sure you have a complete proof of Theorem¬†4.3.1.
    The goal now is to prove Cauchy's Theorem for star-shaped domains, which says that if \(U\) is a star-shaped domain and \(f\) is analytic on \(U\text{,}\) then \(\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 0\) for all closed contours \(\gamma\) in \(U\text{.}\) In light of Proposition¬†4.3.2 and Theorem¬†4.3.1, it suffices to prove the special case where \(\gamma\) is triangular.

    Theorem 4.3.5. Cauchy's Theorem for Triangles.

    Suppose that \(U\) is a domain and that \(f\colon U \to \C\) is analytic on \(U\text{.}\) If \(T\subseteq U\) is a triangle, then \(\oint_{\partial T} f(z)\,dz = 0\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    This is a serious proof! Fix a triangle \(T\) and put \(I = \abs{\int_{\partial T} f(z)\,dz}\text{.}\) Also write \(\ell\) for the perimeter of \(T\text{.}\) Our plan is to bound \(I\text{,}\) showing that \(I \lt \epsilon\) for all \(\epsilon \gt 0\text{.}\) Divide \(T = T^0\) into four subtriangles \(T^0_a, T^0_b, T^0_c, T^0_d\) by taking the midpoints of each of the three sides. As indicated by the picture, these four subtriangles can be oriented in such a way that
    \begin{equation*} \int_{\partial T^0} f = \int_{\partial T^0_a} f + \int_{\partial T^0_b} f + \int_{\partial T^0_c} f + \int_{\partial T^0_d} f \end{equation*}
    It follows that one of the four satisfies \(\abs{\int_{\partial T^0_\bullet f(z)\,dz} \ge \frac{I}{4}\text{.}\) Call that one \(T^1\text{.}\) Notice that the four subtriangles are each similar (in the sense of geometry) to \(T^0\) with edge-lengths halved, so \(\operatorname{perimeter}(T^1) = \frac{\ell}{2}\text{.}\)
    Iterate this process. At stage \(n\) we have \(2^n\) triangles each of perimeter \(\frac{\ell}{2}\) and a particular one \(T^n\) with \(\abs{\int_{\partial T^n} f(z)\,dz} \ge \frac{I}{4^n}\text{.}\) These triangles form a nested sequence \(T^0 \supseteq T^1 \supseteq \cdots\) of closed sets of diameter tending to \(0\text{,}\) so by Compactness there is a unique point \(z_0 \in \bigcap_{m\in\N} T^n\text{.}\) We assumed that \(f\) was analytic at \(z_0\text{,}\) so, fixing arbitrary \(\epsilon > 0\text{,}\) we must have a \(\delta > 0\) for which the following inequality holds for every \(w\in B_\delta(z_0)\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\frac{f(w) - f(z_0)}{w - z_0} - f'(z_0)} \lt \epsilon. \end{equation*}
    Rearrange:
    \begin{equation*} \abs{f(w) - f(z_0) - f'(z_0)(w - z_0)} \lt \epsilon \abs{w - z_0}. \end{equation*}
    Let \(n\) be large enough that \(T^n \subseteq B_\delta(z_0)\text{.}\) We rewrite \(\int_{\partial T^n} f(z)\,dz\) in the following non-obvious way, using the fact that constant functions integrate to \(0\) over closed contours by the FTC.
    \begin{align*} \int_{\partial T^n} f(z)\,dz \amp = \int_{\partial T^n} f(z)\,dz + \int_{\partial T^n} -f(z_0)\,dz + \int_{\partial T^n} -(z-z_0)f'(z_0)\,dz \\ \amp = \int_{\partial T^n} (f(z) - f(z_0) - (z-z_0)f'(z_0))\,dz \end{align*}
    Now we just need to do an ML estimate:
    \begin{align*} \abs{\int_{\partial T^n} f(z)\,dz} \amp \le \int_{\partial T^n} \abs{f(z) - f(z_0) - (z-z_0)f'(z_0)}\,dz \\ \amp \le \int_{\partial T^n} \epsilon \abs{z - z_0} \,dz \\ \amp \le \operatorname{length}(\partial T^n) \cdot \epsilon \sup_{z\in \partial T^n} \abs{z - z_0} \\ \amp \le \epsilon \operatorname{length}(\partial T^n)^2 \end{align*}
    (In the second line we used the fact that \(T^n \subseteq B_\delta(z_0)\text{;}\) in the third we used an ML inequality; and in the fourth we used the fact that the distance between any two points of a triangle is at most its perimeter.)
    Putting everything together, we obtain the following.
    \begin{equation*} I \le 4^n \abs{ \int_{\partial T^n} f(z)\,dz} \le 4^n \epsilon \left( \frac{\ell}{2^n}\right)^2 = \epsilon \ell^2. \end{equation*}
    But \(\epsilon \gt 0\) was arbitrary and \(\ell\) is fixed, so \(I = 0\text{.}\)

    Corollary 4.3.7. Cauchy's Theorem for star-shaped domains.

    Suppose that \(U\) is star-shaped and \(f\colon U \to \C\) is analytic on \(U\text{.}\) Then for any closed contour \(\gamma\) in \(U\text{,}\) we have \(\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 0\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Apply Theorem¬†4.3.5 to conclude that \(f\) integrates to \(0\) over any triangle in \(U\text{.}\) Then apply Proposition¬†4.3.2 to conclude that \(f\) has an antiderivative on \(U\text{.}\) Finally, we can apply Theorem¬†4.3.1 to conclude that for any closed contour \(\gamma\) in \(U\text{,}\) we have \(\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 0\text{.}\)
    NB. Cauchy's Theorem holds for all simply connected domains, not just star-shaped domains. But notice that for example \(\frac{1}{z}\) integrates to a nonzero value over the unit circle, so Cauchy's Theorem does not extend to all domains.
    For a short proof of Cauchy's Theorem that uses Green's Theorem, see Theorem¬†4.4.2.

    Example 4.3.8.

    Suppose that \(R\) is the region bounded between two closed curves \(\gamma_1\) and \(\gamma_2\text{,}\) each oriented counterclockwise. (E.g. \(R\) could be an annulus. See Figure¬†4.3.10.) We will show that if \(f\) is analytic on \(R\) then
    \begin{equation*} \int_{\gamma_1} f(z)\,dz = \int_{\gamma_2} f(z)\,dz. \end{equation*}
    Cut the region by \(\gamma_3\text{,}\) as shown in the figure. The slit region enclosed by the curve \(\gamma_1 + \gamma_3 - \gamma_2 - \gamma_3\) is simply connected, so we can apply Cauchy's Theorem to conclude that
    \begin{equation*} \int_{\gamma_1 + \gamma_3 - \gamma_2 - \gamma_3} f(z)\,dz = 0. \end{equation*}
    But we decompose this using Lemma¬†4.2.2:
    \begin{equation*} \int_{\gamma_1 + \gamma_3 - \gamma_2 - \gamma_3} = \int_{\gamma_1} + \int_{\gamma_3} + \int_{-\gamma_2} + \int_{-\gamma_3} = \int_{\gamma_1} - \int_{\gamma_2} \end{equation*}

    Example 4.3.11.

    Let \(f(z) = \frac{1}{z}\text{,}\) defined on the punctured plane \(\C \wo \{0\}\text{.}\) What values can the integral \(\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz\) take on a simple closed curve \(\gamma\text{?}\)
    Case 1: \(\gamma\) does not encircle \(0\text{.}\) Then there is some curve \(R\) from the origin to \(\infty\) such that \(\gamma\) is completely contained in the slit plane \(\C \wo R\text{,}\) and there is a continuous branch of the log defined on \(\C\wo R\text{.}\) So in this case \(\int_\gamma \frac{1}{z}\,dz = 0\text{,}\) by Theorem¬†4.3.1.
    Case 2: \(\gamma\) encircles \(0\text{.}\) Let \(\gamma_\eps\) be a tiny circle of radius \(\eps\) centered at \(0\text{,}\) so tiny that it lies entirely inside \(\gamma\) (and does not intersect \(\gamma\)). By Example¬†4.3.8 we have \(\int_\gamma \frac{1}{z}\,dz = \int_{\gamma_\eps} \frac{1}{z}\,dz\text{,}\) and the latter integral (over the tiny circle) can be computed directly:
    \begin{align*} \int_\gamma \frac{1}{z}\,dz \amp = \int_{\gamma_\eps} \frac{1}{z}\,dz \\ \amp = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{\epsilon e^{i\theta}}i \eps e^{i\theta} \,d\theta \\ \amp = \int_0^{2\pi} i\,d\theta \\ \amp = 2\pi i \end{align*}
    So for a simple closed curve \(\gamma\text{,}\) the possible values of \(\int_\gamma \frac{1}{z}\,dz \) are \(2\pi i\) and \(0\text{,}\) according to whether \(\gamma\) encircles the origin.
    But a non-simple curve can be divided into simple pieces! And then we can just add up the integrals. So the possible values of \(\int_\gamma \frac{1}{z}\,dz\) are \(2\pi i n\text{,}\) \(n\in\mathbb{Z}\text{.}\) The \(n\) in this formula is called the winding number of \(\gamma\) and is the net number of times that \(\gamma\) crosses the positive \(x\)-axis. (Each time that \(\gamma\) crosses the positive \(x\)-axis from south to north contributes \(+1\text{,}\) each time that \(\gamma\) crosses the positive \(x\)-axis from north to south contributes \(-1\text{.}\))
