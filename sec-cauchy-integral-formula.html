    Section 4.5 Cauchy's Integral Formula

    Theorem 4.5.1.

    Suppose that \(\gamma\) is a simple closed curve and \(f\) is analytic that includes \(\gamma\) and its interior. Assume that \(\gamma\) is counterclockwise-oriented. Then for all \(z_0\) inside \(\gamma\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} f(z_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(z)}{z - z_0} \,dz \end{equation*}
    or sometimes
    \begin{equation*} f(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(w)}{w - z} \, dw \end{equation*}
    Knowing all values of \(f\) on \(\partial D\) gives you all values of \(f\) in \(D\text{!}\) This is an incredible fact, which for the time being we will primarily use to evaluate integrals.

    Example 4.5.2.

    As a reality check, consider \(f(z) = 1\) and let \(\gamma\) parametrize the unit circle counterclockwise.
    \begin{equation*} 1 = f(0) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_{\abs{z} = 1} \frac{1}{z-0}\,dz = \frac{1}{2\pi i} 2\pi i \end{equation*}

    Example 4.5.3.

    Compute \(\int_\gamma \frac{e^{z^2}}{z-2}\,dz\text{,}\) for each of the following \(\gamma\text{.}\)
    First, suppose that \(\gamma\) parametrizes a circle centered at \(z = 2\text{.}\) Notice that \(f(z) = e^{z^2}\) is entire, so the Cauchy Integral Formula gives
    \begin{equation*} e^4 = f(2) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_{\gamma} \frac{e^{z^2}}{z-2}\,dz, \end{equation*}
    which we solve to see that the integral is \(2\pi i e^4\text{.}\)
    Next, suppose that \(\gamma\) is completely included in the first quadrant \(\Re(z) > 0,\) \(\Im(z) > 0\text{.}\) (In fact, the following analysis will apply to any curve \(\gamma\) that does not encircle \(2\text{.}\)) Then the integrand \(\frac{e^{z^2}}{z-2}\) is analytic on (the star-shaped region) \(\set{z}{\Re(z) > 0, \Im(z) > 0}\text{,}\) so Cauchy's Theorem implies that \(\int_\gamma \frac{e^{z^2}}{z-2}\,dz = 0\text{.}\)
    Let \(\gamma\) be the curve that winds clockwise twice around \(2\text{,}\) as pictured. Then we can divide \(\gamma\) into two simple pieces \(\gamma_1\) and \(\gamma_2\) and apply the Cauchy Integral Formula to each piece:
    \begin{equation*} \int_\gamma \frac{e^{z^2}}{z-2}\,dz = \int_{\gamma_1} \frac{f(z)}{z-2}\,dz + \int_{\gamma_2} \frac{f(z)}{z-2}\,dz = -2\pi i f(2) - 2\pi i f(2) = -4 \pi i e^4. \end{equation*}

    Theorem 4.5.4. Improvement of Cauchy's Theorem.

    Suppose that \(D\) is a simply connected domain, \(z_0 \in D\text{,}\) and \(g\) is analytic on \(D \wo \{z_0\}\) and continuous at \(z_0\text{.}\) Then \(\oint_\gamma g(z)\,dz = 0\) for all closed curves \(\gamma\) in \(D\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    (In fact, under these hypotheses, \(g\) must be analytic at \(z_0\text{,}\) as we will see later in Proposition¬†5.2.11.)
    Since \(z_0\) is inside \(\gamma\text{,}\) there is a small \(\eps_0 \gt 0\) such that the disk \(B_{\eps_0}(z_0)\) lies completely inside \(\gamma\text{.}\) Since \(g\) is continuous, \(\abs{g(z)}\) is bounded on the interior of \(\gamma_{\eps_0}\text{;}\) say \(\abs{g(z)} \le M\) on \(B_\eps(z_0)\text{.}\)
    The point of this \(\eps_0\) business is that we need a fixed bound \(M\) in order to conclude that \(ML\) goes to \(0\) as \(\eps\) goes to \(0\text{.}\)
    Consider \(\eps \lt \eps_0\text{.}\) Example¬†4.3.8 implies that \(\oint_\gamma g(z)\,dz = \oint_{\gamma_\eps} g(z)\,dz\text{,}\) where \(\gamma_\eps\) is a circle of radius \(\eps\) centered at \(z_0\text{.}\) Now we just do an ML-estimate:
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\oint_\gamma g(z)\,dz} \le M(2\pi \eps) \underset{\eps \to 0}{\longrightarrow} 0. \end{equation*}
    This completes the proof.

    Proof of the Cauchy Integral Formula.

    Consider the following function.
    \begin{equation*} g(z) = \begin {cases} \frac{f(z) - f(z_0)}{z - z_0} \amp z \ne z_0 \\ f'(z_0) \amp z = z_0 \end {cases} \end{equation*}
    Since \(f\) is differentiable at \(z_0\text{,}\) this function \(g\) is continuous at \(z_0\text{.}\)
    This is a common trick used to prove theorems about derivatives in real analysis.
    By Theorem¬†4.5.4, we have
    \begin{equation*} \oint_\gamma g(z)\,dz = \oint_\gamma \frac{f(z) - f(z_0)}{z - z_0} \,dz = 0. \end{equation*}
    Rearrange this to get
    \begin{equation*} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(z)}{z - z_0} \,dz = \oint_\gamma \frac{f(z_0)}{z - z_0} \, dz = f(z_0) \oint_\gamma \frac{1}{z-z_0}\,dz = f(z_0) \cdot 2\pi i \end{equation*}
    (The last equality follows from our computation of \(\oint \frac{1}{z} \, dz\)) over a circle centered at the origin; do the substitution \(u = z - z_0\text{.}\))

    Theorem 4.5.5. The Cauchy Integral Formula for Derivatives.

    Suppose that \(\gamma\) is a simple closed curve and \(f\) is analytic that includes \(\gamma\) and its interior. Assume that \(\gamma\) is counterclockwise-oriented. Then for all \(z\) inside \(\gamma\text{,}\) the \(n\)th derivative \(f^{(n)}(z)\) exists and satisfies the following formula.
    \begin{equation*} f^{(n)}(z) = \frac{n!}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(w)}{(w - z)^{n+1}} \, dw \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    This is the idea:
    \begin{equation*} f'(z) = \frac{d}{dz} \left( \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(w)}{w - z}\,dw \right) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{d}{dz} \frac{f(w)}{w - z}\,dw = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(w)}{(w - z)^2} \end{equation*}
    And then you iterate to obtain the formula for \(f^{(n)}(z)\text{.}\) But the second equality above requires justification.
    OK, here's what we actually need to do:
    \begin{align*} \frac{f(z+\Delta z) - f(z)}{\Delta z} \amp = \frac{1}{2\pi i \Delta z} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(w)}{w - z - \Delta z} - \frac{f(w)}{w - z} \,dw \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(w)}{(w - z)^2 - \Delta z(w - z)} \, dw \\ \amp \underset{\Delta z \to 0}{\longrightarrow} \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(w)}{(w - z)^2} \, dw \end{align*}
    (There is no problem taking a limit under the integral sign, since everything is continuous and the denominator is nonzero.)

    Corollary 4.5.6.

    If \(f\) is analytic on \(D\text{,}\) then \(f\) has derivatives of all orders on \(D\text{!}\)
    Now let's do several examples.

    Example 4.5.7.

    \begin{equation*} \oint_{\abs{z} = 1} \frac{e^{2z}}{z^4}\,dz = \frac{2\pi i}{3!} \left[\frac{d^3}{d^3z} e^{2z} \right]_{z=0} = \frac{8}{3} \pi i. \end{equation*}

    Example 4.5.8.

    Compute \(\oint_\gamma \frac{1}{(z^2 + 4)^2}\, dz\text{.}\)
    We factor \((z^2 + 4)^2 = (z+2i)^2 (z-2i)^2\) and notice that \(\gamma\) encircles one singularity \(z = 2i\) but not the other. So we define \(f(z) = \frac{1}{(z + 2i)^2}\text{,}\) notice that \(f\) is analytic on \(\gamma\) and its interior, and apply the Cauchy Integral Formula for Derivatives:
    \begin{align*} \oint_\gamma \frac{1}{(z^2 + 4)^2}\,dz \amp = \oint_\gamma \frac{f(z)}{(z - 2i)^2}\,dz \\ \amp = 2\pi i f'(2i) \\ \amp = 2\pi i \left[ \frac{-2}{(z + 2i)^3} \right]_{z = 2i} \\ \amp = \frac{4\pi i}{64 i} = \frac{\pi}{16} \end{align*}

    Example 4.5.10.

    Compute \(\oint_\gamma \frac{z}{z^2 + 4} \,dz\text{,}\) where \(\gamma\) is the curve pictured below.
    Now \(\gamma\) encircles both singularities! So we can't just do what we did in the previous example. So we split the curve in two like this:
    Define \(f_1(z) = \frac{z}{z + 2i}\) and \(f_2(z) = \frac{z}{z-2i}\text{,}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} f(z) = \frac{z}{z^2 + 4} = \frac{f_1(z)}{z - 2i} = \frac{f_2(z)}{z + 2i}. \end{equation*}
    Now we can split up the integral so that we integrate around the two singularities separately:
    \begin{align*} \oint_\gamma \frac{z}{z^2 + 4}\,dz \amp = \oint_{\gamma_1 + \gamma_3 - \gamma_3 + \gamma_2} \frac{z}{z^2 + 4}\,dz \\ \amp = \oint_{\gamma_1 + \gamma_2} \frac{f_1(z)}{z - 2i}\,dz + \oint_{\gamma_2 - \gamma_3} \frac{f_2(z)}{z + 2i}\,dz \\ \amp = 2\pi i(f_1(2i) + f_2(2i)) \\ \amp = 2\pi i \end{align*}
    It is worth noting that we could've used partial fractions instead: \(\frac{z}{(z-2i)(z+2i)} = \frac{a}{z - 2i} + \frac{b}{z + 2i}\text{.}\) Later, we will see how to do this kind of example much more systematically using residue theory.
