Proof

We will do the case where \(\gamma\) encircles two singularities of \(f\text{,}\) at \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) Let \(C_3\) be a tiny circle around \(a\text{,}\) \(C_6\) be a tiny circle around \(b\text{,}\) and connect \(C_6\) and \(C_3\) as in the figure. By Cauchy's Theorem, the function integrates to \(0\) over the big curve:
\begin{equation*} \oint\limits_{C_1 + C_2 - C_3 - C_2 + C_4 + C_5 - C_6 - C_5} f(z)\,dz = 0 = \oint_{C_1 + C_4} f(z)\,dz + \oint_{-C_3 - C_6}f(z)\,dz \end{equation*}
So the integral reduces to the sum of integrals over \(C_3\) and \(C_6\text{,}\) each of which can be computed as \(2\pi i\) times the residue:
\begin{align*} \oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz \amp = \oint_{C_3 + C_6} f(z)\,dz \\ \amp = \oint_{C_3} f(z)\,dz + \oint_{C_6}f(z)\,dz \\ \amp = 2\pi i \Res\limits_{z = b} f(z) + 2\pi i \Res\limits_{z = a} f(z) \\ \amp = 2\pi i\left( \Res\limits_{z = b} f(z) + \Res\limits_{z = a} f(z)\right) \end{align*}
The proof of the general case (with more than two singularities inside \(\gamma\)) is similar.
