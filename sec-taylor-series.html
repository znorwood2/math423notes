    Section 5.1 Taylor Series

    Much of the general theory of series can be imported from real analysis with no material changes.

    Theorem 5.1.1.

    Consider the (complex) power series \(f(z) = \sum_{n\ge 0} c_n (z-a)^n\text{.}\) There is a number \(R \ge 0\text{,}\) called the radius of convergence of \(f\) at \(a\text{,}\) for which the following facts hold.
    1. If \(R \gt 0\) then the series converges absolutely to an analytic function on \(B_R(a)\text{,}\) the disk of convergence of \(f\) at \(a\text{.}\)
      And for \(r \lt R\) the series converges uniformly to its limiting function. You can ignore this if you haven't seen uniform convergence of functions before.
    2. The series diverges for \(\abs{z - a} \gt R\text{.}\)
    3. The derivative \(f'\) is given on \(B_R(a)\) by term-by-term differentiation:
      \begin{equation*} f'(z) = \sum_{n \ge 1} n c_n (z-a)^{n-1} \end{equation*}
      and also has radius of convergence \(R\text{.}\)
    4. We can integrate term by term too. If \(\gamma\) is a contour inside the disk of convergence, then
      \begin{equation*} \int_\gamma f(z)\,dz = \sum_{n\ge 0} \int_\gamma c_n (z - a)^n\,dz. \end{equation*}
    5. In fact the radius of convergence is given by the Root Test:
      \begin{equation*} R = \frac{1}{\limsup \sqrt[n]{\abs{c_n}}}, \end{equation*}
      If you haven't seen limsups before, then take this slightly weaker statement: if \(\lim \sqrt[n]{\abs{c_n}}\) exists, then \(R = \frac{1}{\limsup \sqrt[n]{\abs{c_n}}}\text{.}\)
      (interpreted as \(0\) or \(\infty\) as appropriate).
    NB. Theorem 5.1.1 does not give any general information about the convergence of the power series on the boundary of its disk of convergence, i.e., \(\abs{z - a} = R\text{.}\) Notice also that, unlike in real analysis, the boundary of the disk of convergence includes a whole continuum of points, not just two!

    Theorem 5.1.2. Taylor's Theorem.

    Suppose that \(f\colon B_r(a) \to \C\) is analytic. Then \(f\) has, on \(B_r(a)\text{,}\) a convergent power series expansion
    \begin{equation*} f(z) = \sum_{n\ge 0} c_n (z-a)^n \end{equation*}
    centered at \(a\text{,}\) with coefficients given by
    \begin{equation*} c_n = \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!} = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{\partial B_\rho(a)} \frac{f(w)}{(w-a)^{n+1}}\,dw \end{equation*}
    for any \(\rho \lt r\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Suppose \(\rho \lt r\) and \(z\in B_\rho(a)\text{.}\) Apply the Cauchy Integral Formula:
    \begin{equation} f(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{\partial B_\rho(a)} \frac{f(w)}{w - z} \,dw .\tag{5.1.1} \end{equation}
    Now we use a non-obvious trick. Expand the factor \(\frac{1}{w-z}\) in the integrand into a geometric series, which converges uniformly in \(w \in \partial B_\rho(a)\) since \(\abs{\frac{z-a}{w-a}} = \frac{\abs{z-a}}{\rho} \lt 1\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{w-z} = \frac{1}{(w-a)\left( 1 - \frac{z-a}{w-a} \right)} = \frac{1}{w-a} \sum_{n\ge 0} \frac{(z-a)^n}{(w-a)^{n}} = \sum_{n\ge 0} \frac{(z-a)^n}{(w-a)^{n+1}} \end{equation*}
    Using this to replace the \(\frac{1}{w-z}\) in (5.1.1) with a series and using the uniformity of the convergence to interchange a sum and an integral gives the following.
    \begin{align*} f(z) \amp = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{\partial B_\rho(a)} \frac{f(w)}{w - z}\,dz \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{\partial B_{\rho(a)}} \sum_{n\ge 0} \frac{f(w) (z-a)^n}{(w-a)^{n+1}}\,dw \\ \amp = \sum_{n\ge 0} \left( \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{\partial B_{\rho}(a)} \frac{f(w)}{(w-a)^{n+1}}\,dw \right) (z-a)^n\\ \amp = \sum_{n\ge 0} c_n (z-a)^n \end{align*}
    This is exactly what we wanted to show.

    Corollary 5.1.3.

    If \(\sum_{n\ge 0} c_n (z-a)^n\) and \(\sum_{n\ge 0} d_n (z-a)^n\) are two power series that converge to the same function on an open disk centered at \(a\text{,}\) then \(c_n = d_n\) for all \(n\ge 0\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    (This is not really a corollary to the previous theorem. But it is important to note that the Taylor coefficients given in that theorem are unique.)
    If the two series converge to \(f\) on \(B_r(a)\text{,}\) then (exercise) they are analytic there. (You can differentiate term by term.) So we must have \(f(a) = c_0 = d_0\text{,}\) \(f'(a) = c_1 = d_1\text{,}\) \(f''(a) = 2c_2 = 2d_2\text{,}\) etc. (Proceed by induction.)

    Corollary 5.1.4.

    If \(f\) is analytic on a domain, then it is infinitely differentiable on that domain.

    Corollary 5.1.5. Morera's Theorem.

    This is a nice part of the theory, but we are much more interested in theorems that tell us facts about analytic functions than in those that tell us when a function is analytic.
    If \(f\) is continuous on a domain \(U\) and \(\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 0\) for all closed contours \(\gamma\) in \(U\text{,}\) then \(f\) is analytic on \(U\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    We can apply Theorem 4.3.1 to conclude that \(f\) has an antiderivative \(F\) on \(U\text{.}\) But then \(F\) is infinitely differentiable on \(U\text{,}\) so \(f\) is analytic on \(U\text{.}\)

    Corollary 5.1.6.

    If \(f\) is analytic on a domain \(U\) and \(z_0 \in U\text{,}\) then \(f\) has a power series expansion centered at \(z_0\) with radius of convergence at least the distance from \(z_0\) to \(\partial U\text{.}\)
    The distance from a point \(z\) to a closed set \(G\) is defined to be the minimum \(\min\set{ \abs{w - z} }{ w \in G}\) when this minimum exists. Notice that if \(z \notin G\text{,}\) then the function \(w \mapsto \abs{w - z}\) is continuous and takes only positive values so must achieve a positive minimum value if \(G\) is compact (= closed and bounded).

    Example 5.1.7.

    Let's compute the Taylor series expansion of the exponential function \(f(z) = e^z\) centered at \(a = 0\text{.}\) Since the exponential map is entire, we know in advance that the Taylor series will have infinite radius of convergence.
    All derivatives of \(f\) are the same: \(f(z) = f'(z) = \cdots = e^z\text{.}\) So the \(n\)th Taylor coefficient is \(\frac{f^{(n)}(0)}{n!} = \frac{1}{n!}\text{,}\) and the series is given by
    \begin{equation*} e^z = 1 + z + \frac{z^2}{2!} + \frac{z^3}{3!} + \cdots = \sum_{n\ge 0} \frac{z^n}{n!}. \end{equation*}
    Great, this is the same as the series for the real exponential! This is not a coincidence.

    Example 5.1.8.

    Compute the Taylor series expansion of \(f(z) = z^8 e^{3z}\) around \(a = 0\text{.}\)
    Just plug \(3z\) in for \(z\) in the Taylor series for \(e^z\) and multiply by \(z^8\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} e^{3z} \amp = \sum_{n\ge 0} \frac{3^n}{n!} z^n \\ f(z) \amp = \sum_{n\ge 0} \frac{3^n}{n!} z^{n+8} \end{align*}

    Example 5.1.9.

    Compute the power series expansion of \(f(z) = \sin(z)\) centered around \(a = 0\text{.}\)
    Method 1: Compute derivatives of \(\sin(z)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} f^{(n)}(0) = \frac{d^n \sin(z)}{dz^n} = \begin {cases} (-1)^m \amp \text{ if } n = 2m+1 \\ 0 \amp \text{ if }n \text{ is even } \end {cases} \end{equation*}
    Then use the formula.
    Method 2: Recall that \(\sin(z) = \frac{e^{iz} - e^{-iz}}{2i}\text{,}\) so we can use the series for \(e^z\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \sin(z) \amp = \frac{1}{2i} \left[ \sum_{n\ge 0} \frac{(iz)^n}{n!} - \sum_{n\ge 0} \frac{(-iz)^n}{n!} \right] \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2i} \sum_{n\ge 0} (1 - (-1)^n) \frac{i^n z^n}{n!} \\ \amp = \sum_{k\ge 0} (-1)^k \frac{z^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!} \end{align*}
    This series converges for all \(z\text{.}\) (This follows either from the fact that \(\sin(z)\) is entire, or from the fact that we built the series from the Taylor series for \(e^z\text{,}\) which converges everywhere.)

    Example 5.1.10.

    Compute the Taylor series expansion for \(f(z) = \frac{1}{1 - z}\) around \(a = 5\text{.}\) We should observe in advance that this series will have radius of convergence \(4\text{,}\) the distance to the nearest singularity at \(z = 1\text{!}\)
    You can compute derivatives and use the formula, but it is easier to just recognize that we will get a geometric series and use an algebraic trick:
    \begin{equation*} f(z) = \frac{1}{-4(1 + \frac{z-5}{4})} = \frac{-1}{4} \left( 1 - \left( \frac{z-5}{4} \right) + \left( \frac{z-5}{4} \right)^2 - \left( \frac{z-5}{4} \right)^3 + \cdots \right) \end{equation*}
    This series converges iff \(\abs{\frac{z-5}{4}}\lt 1\text{,}\) i.e., when \(\abs{z - 5} \lt 4\text{,}\) as predicted.

    Example 5.1.11.

    Compute the Taylor series expansion of \(f(z) = \Log(1+z)\) centered at \(a = 0\text{.}\)
    We first observe that the branch cut starts at \(z = -1\text{,}\) which is the nearest point (to \(0\)) at which \(f\) is not analytic. So the radius of convergence is \(1\text{.}\)
    Next we observe that the derivative of \(f\) has a geometric series expansion:
    \begin{equation*} f'(z) = \frac{1}{1 + z} = 1 - z + z^2 - z^3 + \cdots \end{equation*}
    Integrating term by term gives a series expansion for \(f\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} f(z) \amp = a_0 + z - \frac{z^2}{2} + \frac{z^3}{3} - \frac{z^4}{4} + \cdots \\ \amp = a_0 + \sum_{n\ge 1} (-1)^{n+1} \frac{z^n}{n} \end{align*}
    We finish the computation by observing that \(f(0) = a_0 = \Log(1) = 0\text{.}\)

    Example 5.1.12.

    Consider the function \(f(x) = \frac{1}{1 + x^2}\text{,}\) a beautiful function of a real variable. It has a Taylor series expansion centered at any \(a\in \R\text{,}\) yet the radius of convergence of its series expansion at \(a = 0\) is \(1\text{.}\) Why?! There is no explanation of this in real calculus.
    (Just to refresh your memory:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{1+x^2} = \sum_{n\ge 0} (-x^2)^n = 1 - x^2 + x^4 - x^6 + \cdots \end{equation*}
    converges when \(\abs{x^2} \lt 1\text{,}\) i.e., \(\abs{x} \lt 1\text{.}\))
    But now we see what's going on! As a function of a complex variable, this function has singularities at \(\pm i\text{,}\) and \(1\) is the distance from \(0\) to the nearest singularity.
