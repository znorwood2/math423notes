This is the idea:
\begin{equation*} f'(z) = \frac{d}{dz} \left( \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(w)}{w - z}\,dw \right) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{d}{dz} \frac{f(w)}{w - z}\,dw = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(w)}{(w - z)^2} \end{equation*}
And then you iterate to obtain the formula for \(f^{(n)}(z)\text{.}\) But the second equality above requires justification.
OK, here's what we actually need to do:
\begin{align*} \frac{f(z+\Delta z) - f(z)}{\Delta z} \amp = \frac{1}{2\pi i \Delta z} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(w)}{w - z - \Delta z} - \frac{f(w)}{w - z} \,dw \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(w)}{(w - z)^2 - \Delta z(w - z)} \, dw \\ \amp \underset{\Delta z \to 0}{\longrightarrow} \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(w)}{(w - z)^2} \, dw \end{align*}
(There is no problem taking a limit under the integral sign, since everything is continuous and the denominator is nonzero.)
