We can find the Laurent series of the function \(\frac{z}{z^2 + 1}\) using partial fractions:
\begin{equation*} f(z) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{z-i} + \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{z+i} \end{equation*}
Note that \(\frac{1}{z+i}\) is analytic at \(z=i\) and has Taylor series expansion that can be found using geometric series:
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{z+i} = \frac{1}{2i} \frac{1}{1 + \frac{z-i}{2i}} = \frac{1}{2i} \sum_{n\ge 0} \left(- \frac{z-i}{2i}\right)^n \end{equation*}
The Laurent series is
\begin{equation*} f(z) = \frac12 \cdot \frac{1}{z-i} + \frac{1}{4i} \sum_{n\ge 0} \left( - \frac{z-i}{2i} \right)^n \end{equation*}
and converges for \(0 \lt \abs{z-i} \lt 2\text{.}\)
