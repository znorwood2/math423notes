As we've seen before, the complex zeros of sine occur at \(n\pi\) for \(n\) an integer. Since the derivative \(\sin'(n\pi) = \cos(n\pi)\) is nonzero at each pole, the zeros of sine are simple. So, by the Proposition,
\begin{equation*} \Res\limits_{z=n\pi} f(z) = \frac{1}{\cos(n\pi)} = (-1)^n\text{.} \end{equation*}
