    Section 4.6 Applications of the Cauchy Integral Formula

    Theorem 4.6.1.

    Write \(C_R\) for the circle of radius \(R\) centered at \(z_0\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f\) is analytic on \(C_R\) and its interior, and put \(M_R = \max\limits_{z\in C_R} \abs{f(z)}\text{.}\) Then, for \(n =1,2,3,\dots\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \abs{ f^{(n)}(z_0)} \le \frac{n! M_R}{R^n}. \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    This is just a matter of combining the Cauchy Integral Formula for Derivatives with an ML-estimate:
    \begin{align*} \abs{f^{(n)}(z_0)} \amp = \abs{\frac{n!}{2\pi i} \oint_{C_R} \frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}} \, dz} \\ \amp \le \frac{n!}{2\pi} \oint_{C_R} \frac{\abs{f(z)}}{\abs{z-z_0}^{n+1}}\,\abs{dz} \\ \amp \le \frac{n!}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{M_R}{R^{n+1}} \oint_{C_R} \abs{dz} \\ \amp = \frac{n!}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{M_R}{R^{n+1}} \cdot 2\pi R \\ \amp = \frac{n! M_R}{R^n} \end{align*}

    Definition 4.6.2.

    A function \(f\colon D\to\C\) is bounded if there is a real number \(B\) for which \(\abs{f(z)} \le B\) for all \(z\in D\text{.}\)

    Theorem 4.6.3. Liouville's Theorem.

    A bounded entire function must be constant.

    Proof.

    Suppose that \(\abs{f(z)} \lt M\) on all of \(\C\text{.}\) Then for every circle \(C_R\text{,}\) we have by Theorem¬†4.6.1
    \begin{equation*} \abs{f'(z_0)} \le \frac{M}{R} \end{equation*}
    But then \(M\) is fixed, and we could have chosen \(R\) to be as large as we like, so \(f'(z_0) = 0\text{.}\) So Lemma¬†3.2.3 implies that \(f\) must be constant.
    NB. Polynomials, \(e^z\text{,}\) sine, cosine (!), etc., are all entire but not bounded.

    Corollary 4.6.4. The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.

    Every nonconstant polynomial \(p(z)\) with complex coefficients has a root in \(\C\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Write
    \begin{equation*} p(z) = a_n z^n + a_{n-1} z^{n-1} + \cdots + a_1 z + a_0. \end{equation*}
    Suppose toward a contradiction that \(p(z)\) does not have a complex root, so that \(\frac{1}{p(z)}\) is entire.

    Claim 4.6.5.

    \(\frac{1}{p(z)}\) is bounded.

    Proof.

    This is the idea:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{p(z)}{z^n} = a_n + \frac{a_{n-1}}{z} + \frac{a_{n-2}}{z^2} + \cdots + \frac{a_0}{z^n} \underset{\abs{z}\to\infty}{\longrightarrow} a_n. \end{equation*}
    Here is a bit more detail. Fixing \(\eps \gt 0\text{,}\) find an \(R \gt 0\) for which the inequality
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\frac{p(z)}{z^n} - a_n} = \abs{ \frac{a_{n-1}}{z} + \frac{a_{n-2}}{z^2} + \cdots + \frac{a_0}{z^n} } \lt \eps \end{equation*}
    holds for all \(z\) with \(\abs{z} \gt R\text{.}\) Notice that now \(\abs{z} \gt R\) implies \(\abs{\frac{1}{p(z)}} \lt \frac{1}{\abs{a_n} R^n} + \eps\text{.}\) We have found a bound for \(\frac{1}{p(z)}\) for \(\abs{z} \gt R\text{.}\) Now remember that \(z\mapsto \abs{\frac{1}{p(z)}}\) is a continuous function on the compact ball \(\abs{z} \le R\) and is therefore bounded. Combine these two bounds to see that \(\frac{1}{p(z)}\) is bounded on \(\C\text{.}\)
    Now apply Theorem¬†4.6.3 to see that \(\frac{1}{p(z)}\) must be constant. So \(p(z)\) must be constant, oops.

    Theorem 4.6.6. The Mean Value Property for Analytic Functions.

    The value of an analytic function \(f\) at \(z_0\) is the average value of \(f\) on a small circle centered at \(z_0\text{.}\)
    More precisely, suppose that \(f\) is analytic on a domain that includes the closed disk \(\abs{z - z_0} \le r\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} f(z_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} f(z_0 + re^{i\theta})\,d\theta. \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    This is just what you get by applying the Cauchy Integral Formula to \(C_r\text{,}\) the circle of radius \(r\) centered at \(z_0\text{,}\) and parametrizing the circle to compute the integral:
    \begin{align*} f(z_0) \amp = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{C_r} \frac{f(z)}{z-z_0} \,dz \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{f(z+re^{i\theta})}{re^{i\theta}} ire^{i\theta}\,d\theta \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} f(z+re^{i\theta})\,d\theta \end{align*}
    This completes the proof.

    Subsection 4.6.1 The Maximum Modulus Principle

    Lemma 4.6.8.

    Suppose that \(f\) is analytic on \(B_r(z_0)\) and \(\abs{f}\) achieves its maximum at \(z_0\text{.}\) Then \(f\) must be constant on \(B_r(z_0)\text{.}\)
    So for example an open disk cannot be mapped by \(f\) to a non-open set. But this is for a more fundamental reason: nonconstant analytic functions are open mappings.

    Proof.

    Fix \(\eps \lt r\text{.}\) Use the Mean Value Property and the fact that \(\abs{f}\) achieves its maximum at \(z_0\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \abs{f(z_0)} \amp = \abs{ \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} f(z_0 + \eps e^{i\theta})\,d\theta } \\ \amp \le \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} \abs{f(z_0 + \eps e^{i\theta})}\,d\theta \\ \amp \le \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} \abs{f(z_0)}\,d\theta \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2\pi} 2\pi \abs{f(z_0)} \end{align*}
    So \(\abs{f(z_0)} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} \abs{f(z_0 + \eps e^{i\theta})}\,d\theta\text{,}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^{2\pi} \abs{f(z_0+\eps e^{i\theta})} - \abs{f(z_0)}\,d\theta = 0. \end{equation*}
    The integrand \(\abs{f(z_0+\eps e^{i\theta})} - \abs{f(z_0)}\) is a continuous nonnegative real-valued function on \([0,2\pi]\) that integrates to \(0\text{.}\) Thus this function must be \(0\text{,}\) by a standard argument in real analysis. So \(\abs{f(z_0)} = \abs{f(z_0 + \eps e^{i\theta})}\) for all \(\theta \in [0,2\pi]\text{.}\)
    Remember that \(\eps \lt r\) was arbitrary. So \(\abs f\) is constant on \(B_r(z_0)\text{.}\) So \(f\) must be constant on \(B_r(z_0)\text{,}\) by Corollary¬†3.2.6.

    Theorem 4.6.9. The Maximum Modulus Principle.

    If \(f\) is analytic on a domain \(D\) and \(\abs{f}\) achieves its maximum value on \(D\text{,}\) i.e., there is \(z_0 \in D\) such that \(\abs{f(z)} \le \abs{f(z_0)}\) for all \(z\in D\text{,}\) then \(f\) must be constant.

    Proof.

    Use Lemma¬†4.6.8 and polygonal paths, imitating part of the proof of Lemma¬†3.2.3.

    Corollary 4.6.10.

    Suppose that \(f\) is continuous on a closed bounded region \(R\) and analytic in the interior of \(R\text{.}\) Then either \(f\) is constant on \(R\text{,}\) or \(\abs{f}\) achieves its maximum (as it must!) on \(\partial R\) and never in the interior of \(R\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    This is just because the interior of \(R\) is a domain. Just apply the previous theorem.

    Example 4.6.11.

    Find the maximum modulus of \(e^z\) on the unit square \([0,1]\times[0,1]\text{.}\)
    Notice that \(\abs{e^z} = \abs{e^{x + iy}} = e^x\) is maximal when \(x = 1\text{,}\) on the boundary of the square.
