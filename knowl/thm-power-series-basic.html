Theorem 5.1.1.

Consider the (complex) power series \(f(z) = \sum_{n\ge 0} c_n (z-a)^n\text{.}\) There is a number \(R \ge 0\text{,}\) called the radius of convergence of \(f\) at \(a\text{,}\) for which the following facts hold.
  1. If \(R \gt 0\) then the series converges absolutely to an analytic function on \(B_R(a)\text{,}\) the disk of convergence of \(f\) at \(a\text{.}\)
    And for \(r \lt R\) the series converges uniformly to its limiting function. You can ignore this if you haven't seen uniform convergence of functions before.
  2. The series diverges for \(\abs{z - a} \gt R\text{.}\)
  3. The derivative \(f'\) is given on \(B_R(a)\) by term-by-term differentiation:
    \begin{equation*} f'(z) = \sum_{n \ge 1} n c_n (z-a)^{n-1} \end{equation*}
    and also has radius of convergence \(R\text{.}\)
  4. We can integrate term by term too. If \(\gamma\) is a contour inside the disk of convergence, then
    \begin{equation*} \int_\gamma f(z)\,dz = \sum_{n\ge 0} \int_\gamma c_n (z - a)^n\,dz. \end{equation*}
  5. In fact the radius of convergence is given by the Root Test:
    \begin{equation*} R = \frac{1}{\limsup \sqrt[n]{\abs{c_n}}}, \end{equation*}
    If you haven't seen limsups before, then take this slightly weaker statement: if \(\lim \sqrt[n]{\abs{c_n}}\) exists, then \(R = \frac{1}{\limsup \sqrt[n]{\abs{c_n}}}\text{.}\)
    (interpreted as \(0\) or \(\infty\) as appropriate).
