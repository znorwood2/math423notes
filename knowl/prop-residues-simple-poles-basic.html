Proposition 6.1.13. Basic properties of residues at simple poles.

  1. If the Laurent series of \(f\) near \(a\) takes the form
    \begin{equation*} \frac{c_{-1}}{z-a} + c_0 + c_1(z-a) + \cdots, \end{equation*}
    then \(f\) has a simple pole at \(z = a\) and \(\Res\limits_{z=a}f(z) = c_{-1}\text{.}\)
  2. If \(g(z) = (z-a)f(z)\) is analytic at \(a\text{,}\) then \(f\) has either a removable singularity or a simple pole at \(z = a\text{.}\) In either case, \(\Res\limits_{z=a}f(z) = g(a)\) (\(=0\) if \(f\) has a removable singularity).
  3. If \(f\) has a simple pole at \(a\) then
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{z\to a} (z-a)f(z) = \Res\limits_{z=a}f(z). \end{equation*}
    (That is, the limit exists and equals the residue. The converse is also true: if the limit exists then \(f\) either has a simple pole at \(a\) or is analytic at \(a\text{.}\))
  4. If \(f\) has a simple pole at \(a\) and \(g\) is analytic at \(a\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \Res\limits_{z=a} f(z) g(z) = g(a) \Res\limits_{z=a} f(z). \end{equation*}
    If \(g(a) \ne 0\) then also
    \begin{equation*} \Res\limits_{z=a} \frac{f(z)}{g(z)} = \frac{1}{g(a)} \Res_{z=a} f(z). \end{equation*}
  5. If \(g\) has a simple zero at \(a\) then \(1/g\) has a simple pole at \(a\) and
    \begin{equation*} \Res_{z=a} \frac{1}{g(z)} = \frac{1}{g'(a)}. \end{equation*}
