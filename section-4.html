    Section 1.4 Some elementary functions

    The rest of the course will be devoted to studying functions of a complex variable, that is, functions \(f\colon D\to\C\) where \(D \subseteq \C\text{.}\) Because \(\C\) is inherently 2-dimensional, we cannot reasonably hope to graph a complex-valued function in the way that we did for functions \(\R\to\R\) in calculus. Instead, we think of complex-valued functions geometrically as transformations of the plane. In trying to understand a particular function, we will pay special attention to how familiar geometric shapes like lines and circles are transformed.

    Subsection 1.4.1 Multiplication and addition by a fixed number

    The most basic complex-valued functions simply take a \(z\in\C\) and add to it, or multiply it by, a fixed complex number \(z_0\text{.}\)
    Addition by a fixed complex number \(z_0\) is just vector-translation. Remember that addition of complex numbers is just vector addition from multivariable calculus.
    Multiplication by a fixed complex number \(z_0 = re^{i\theta}\) is best thought of in polar coordinates, as the composite of two transformations. Notice that \(z\mapsto rz\text{,}\) for \(r\in\R\text{,}\) just scales every point in the plane by a factor of \(r\text{.}\) And the map \(z\mapsto e^{i\theta}z\text{,}\) for a fixed \(\theta\in\R\text{,}\) rotates the plane counterclockwise by \(\theta\) radians. So the map \(z\mapsto re^{i\theta}z\) is the composite of these two maps: it scales by a factor of \(r\) and rotates by \(\theta\) radians.

    Exploration 1.4.1.

    If you have seen some linear algebra, it is worth noting that scaling and rotating are linear maps! And you can analyze them using the tools of linear algebra. To do this, we first observe that the standard basis vectors \(\begin {bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end {bmatrix}\) and \(\begin {bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end {bmatrix}\) have (respectively) representations \(1 = 1+0i\) and \(i = 0+1i\) as complex numbers. Since the maps \(z\mapsto rz\) and \(z\mapsto e^{i\theta}z\) are both linear, their standard matrix representations can be found by looking at where they send the standard basis vectors.
    The more interesting of the two maps is the rotation map \(z\mapsto e^{i\theta}z\text{,}\) so let's see where it sends the standard basis vectors:
    \begin{align*} 1\cdot e^{i\theta} \amp = e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta \\ i\cdot e^{i\theta} \amp = e^{i\pi/2}e^{i\theta} = \cos(\theta+\tfrac{\pi}{2}) + i\sin(\theta+\tfrac{\pi}{2}) = -\sin(\theta) + i\cos(\theta) \end{align*}
    These two results give us the columns of the standard matrix of the linear transformation \(1\cdot e^{i\theta} = e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta \text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta\\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    You might recognize this from linear algebra as a rotation matrix.

    Subsection 1.4.2 The Square Function

    One of the simplest nontrivial functions is the squaring function \(f(z) = z^2\text{.}\) As we will see in many of these examples, it is useful to consider the map in polar coordinates.
    \begin{equation*} f(z) = z^2 = (re^{i\theta})^2 = r^2 e^{i\cdot 2\theta}. \end{equation*}
    In other words, the function \(f\) is defined by declaring \(\abs{f(z)} = \abs{z}^2\) and \(\arg f(z) = 2\arg z\text{.}\)
    The circle \(\abs{z} = r_0\) is mapped to the circle \(\abs{z} = r_0^2\text{,}\) traversed at double the angular velocity. In particular, the unit circle \(\abs{z} = 1\) is mapped to itself, but traversed twice.

    Subsection 1.4.3 The Square Root Function

    Every nonzero complex number should have two square-roots. The problem is that we want to choose one in a reasonable way. (We cannot simply choose the 'positive' one as we can with real numbers.) Looking in polar coordinates suggests that we should define
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{z} = \sqrt{re^{i\theta}} = \sqrt{r} e^{i\theta/2}. \end{equation*}
    (Notice that the square-root on the right is the ordinary positive square-root of the nonnegative real number \(r\text{.}\))
    But a complex number \(z\) has many arguments \(\theta\text{!}\) The trouble comes from picking a particular \(\theta\text{.}\)
    As you can see in Figure¬†1.4.5, using the principal argument for \(\theta\) introduces a discontinuity at every point along the negative real axis. To fix this, we take a branch cut.

    Definition 1.4.6.

    The principal branch of the square-root function is the function \(\C \wo (-\infty,0] \to \C\) defined by \(\sqrt{z} = \sqrt{\abs{z}} e^{i\Arg{z}/2}\text{.}\)
    Warning: Negative real numbers still have complex square roots!!! They are excluded from the domain of the principal branch of the square-root function only to ensure that the function is continuous. More about this later.
    (In fact, the formula in Definition¬†1.4.6 can be used to get a square-root of a negative real number. Plugging in \(z = -1 = 1\cdot e^{i\pi}\text{,}\) we obtain \(e^{i\pi / 2} = i\) as a square-root of \(-1\text{.}\))
    So the two square-roots of a complex number \(z = re^{i\Arg(z)}\) are \(\sqrt r e^{i\Arg(z)/2}\) and \(-\sqrt r e^{i\Arg(z)/2}\text{.}\) (The first of these is the principal branch of the square-root, as long as \(z\notin (-\infty,0]\text{.}\))

    Example 1.4.7.

    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{i} = \sqrt{e^{i\pi/2}} = e^{i\pi/4} = \frac{1}{\sqrt2} + \frac{1}{\sqrt2}i. \end{equation*}

    Subsection 1.4.4 The Complex Exponential

    Euler's Formula tells us how to exponentiate so-called purely imaginary numbers, that is numbers of the form \(i y\) where \(y\in\R\text{.}\) But what about other complex numbers? Well, just decompose \(z\) as \(x+iy\text{,}\) the sum of a real number \(x\) (which we know how to exponentiate) and a purely imaginary number \(iy\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} e^z = e^{x+iy} = e^x e^{iy} = e^x\cos(y) + ie^x \sin(y). \end{equation*}
    In other words, \(e^{x}e^{iy}\) is the polar representation of \(e^z\text{:}\) \(\abs{e^z} = e^x = e^{\Re z}\) and \(\arg(e^z) = y = \Im(z)\text{.}\)

    Observation 1.4.8.

    Definition 1.4.9.

    A complex number \(\lambda\) is a period of the function \(f\colon\C\to\C\) if, for every \(z\in\C\text{,}\) we have \(f(z) = f(z+\lambda)\text{.}\) A function \(f\) is periodic if it has some nonzero period.

    Proposition 1.4.10.

    The complex exponential \(z\mapsto e^z\) is \(2\pi i\)-periodic.

    Proof.

    For all \(z = x+iy \in \C\) we have:
    \begin{align*} e^{z+2\pi i} \amp = e^{x + i(y+2\pi)} \\ \amp = e^x (\cos(y+2\pi) + i\sin(y+2\pi)) \\ \amp = e^x (cos(y) + i\sin(y)) \\ \amp = e^{x+iy} = e^z \end{align*}

    Example 1.4.11.

    For every real number \(r\text{,}\) the quantity \(e^{r+2\pi i} = e^r\) is a real number.
    We conclude from our previous discussion that the range of the exponential map \(z\mapsto e^z\) is \(\C\wo\{0\}\text{.}\) This leads us to wonder about defining a logarithm.

    Subsection 1.4.5 The complex logarithm

    We would like to define a log map \(\C\wo\{0\} \to \C\) that is inverse to the exponential. As we found with the square-root, we will have to make a branch cut in order to choose a specific log continuously, though.
    Given \(z \ne 0\text{,}\) we want to find a solution to the equation \(z = e^w = e^{\Re w} e^{i\Im w}\text{.}\) We need \(\abs{z} = e^{\Re w}\) and \(\Im w = \arg z\text{.}\) In fact we must have this; all logarithms of \(z\) take the form
    \begin{equation*} \log z = \ln \abs z + i \arg z \end{equation*}
    for some choice of \(\arg z\text{.}\) So all logarithms of \(z\) take the form
    \begin{equation*} \log z = \ln \abs z + i\Arg z + i(2\pi m), \quad m\in\mathbb{Z}. \end{equation*}

    Definition 1.4.13.

    The principal branch of the log is the function \(\Log \colon \C\wo(-\infty,0]\to \C\) defined by \(\Log(z) = \ln\abs{z} + i\Arg z\text{.}\)
    As with the square-root, we have to cut the domain in order for the function \(\Log\) to be continuous. And, as with the square-root, negative real numbers still have logarithms!

    Example 1.4.15.

    \begin{equation*} \Log(1+i) = \Log(\sqrt2 e^{i \pi / 4}) = \ln\sqrt2 + i\pi/4 \approx 0.35 + i\pi/4 \end{equation*}
    All possible logs of \(1+i\) are \(\log(1+i) = \ln\sqrt2 + i\pi/4 + 2\pi m i\text{,}\) \(m\in\mathbb{Z}\text{.}\)

    Subsection 1.4.6 Branch cuts and branch points

    We can view the log, for example, as a multi-valued function on \(\C\wo\{0\}\text{.}\) Each time we use log, we need to choose a branch (often the principal one).

    Definition 1.4.16.

    If \(U\subseteq\C\wo\{0\}\) is an open set, then a branch of the log defined on \(U\) is a continuous function \(f\colon U\to\C\) for which \(e^{f(z)} = z\) for all \(z\in U\text{.}\)
    A branch point of a multi-valued function \(f\) is a point \(p\in\C\) such that, for every \(\epsilon \gt 0\text{,}\) the function cannot be given a continuous single-valued definition on \(B_\epsilon(p) \wo \{p\}\text{.}\)
    \(B_\epsilon(p) = \set{ z\in\C }{ \abs{z - p} \lt \epsilon\) is the ball of radius \(\epsilon\) centered at \(p\text{.}\) Don't worry, we'll discuss these things in more detail in later sections.

    Example 1.4.17.

    \(0\) is a branch point of log.

    Example 1.4.18.

    The function \(f(z) = \sqrt{z(z-1)}\) has two branch points, at \(z = 0\) and \(z = 1\text{.}\)

    Subsection 1.4.7 General power functions

    Now that we have the log, we can define general power functions.

    Definition 1.4.19.

    For any \(\alpha\in\mathbb{Z}\text{,}\) the principal branch of the power function \(z\mapsto z^\alpha\) is the function \(\C\wo (-\infty,0] \to \C\) defined as follows.
    \begin{equation*} z^\alpha = e^{\alpha \Log(z)} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.4.20.

    This agrees with our earlier definition of the principal branch of the square-root function:
    \begin{equation*} z^{1/2} = e^{\frac12 \Log z} = e^{\frac12 \ln \abs{z} + \frac12 i \Arg z} = \abs{z}^{1/2} e^{i\Arg z / 2} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.4.21.

    Let's find all possible values of \(i^i\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} i^i = e^{i \log i} = e^{i(\ln 1 + i \arg i)} = e^{-\arg i}. \end{equation*}
    Since the possible values of \(\arg i\) are \(\frac{\pi}{2} + 2\pi m\text{,}\) \(m\in\mathbb{Z}\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} i^i = e^{-\pi/2} = e^{-5\pi / 2} = \cdots \end{equation*}
    These are all real numbers!!!

    Subsection 1.4.8 Trig functions

    Standard trig functions can be defined in terms of the exponential.

    Definition 1.4.22.

    Sine and cosine are defined at all complex numbers \(z\) as follows.
    \begin{equation*} \cos(z) = \frac{e^{iz} + e^{-iz}}{2} \quad \sin(z) = \frac{e^{iz} - e^{-iz}}{2i} \end{equation*}
    Remember your Taylor Series from calculus? Write down the Taylor series for the exponential function, centered at \(0\text{,}\) and plug in \(iz\) and simplify. Do the same thing but plug in \(-iz\) and simplify. You should be very close to a Taylor series for cosine.
    Warning: These definitions are motivated by Euler's Formula: e.g. for \(x\in\R\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \cos(x) = \Re(e^{ix}) = \frac{e^{ix} + e^{-ix}}{2}. \end{equation*}
    But \(\cos(z) \ne \Re(e^{iz})\) in general! In particular, the quantity on the right is real-valued, whereas \(\cos(z)\) is not. And we can just expand out \(e^{iz}\) and examine its real and imaginary parts:
    \begin{equation*} e^{iz} = e^{i(x+iy)} = e^{-y + ix} = e^{-y}(\cos x + i\sin x). \end{equation*}
    So \(\Re(e^{iz}) = e^{-y}\cos x\text{,}\) which does not typically equal \(\cos z\text{.}\)
    The tangent function can be defined by \(\tan z = \frac{\sin z}{\cos z}\text{,}\) and hyperbolic trig functions can be defined similarly. You will explore all this on the homework.
