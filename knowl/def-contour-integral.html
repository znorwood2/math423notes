Definition 4.1.3.

We first define the complex integral for paths in the complex plane. If \(g\colon [a,b]\to \C\) is continuous, then we integrate \(g\) by integrating its real and imaginary parts separately:
\begin{equation*} \int_a^b g(t)\,dt = \int_a^b \Re(g(t))\,dt + i \int_a^b \Im(g(t))\,dt. \end{equation*}
Notice that the result is a complex number. But this is really just a prelude to the main event:
For a \(C^1\) path \(\gamma\colon[a,b]\to U\subseteq \C\) and a continuous function \(f\colon U\to \C\text{,}\) we define the integral of \(f\) over \(\gamma\) as follows.
\begin{equation*} \int_\gamma f(z)\, dz = \int_a^b f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)\,dt. \end{equation*}
Notice that the integral on the righthand side is defined by the first part of this definition (with \(g(t) = f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)\)) to be
\begin{equation*} \int_a^b \Re(f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t))\,dt + i\int_a^b \Im(f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t))\,dt. \end{equation*}
The definition naturally extends to contours \(\gamma\) by adding integrals over \(C^1\) pieces.
